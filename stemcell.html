Stem Cell Therapy

A complete guide to regenerative cellular medicine

Swiss Regenerative Klinik · Zurich

Introduction

Stem cells are among the most remarkable tools in modern medicine. As the body's raw material, they hold the ability to repair, replace, and regenerate damaged tissue — offering a way to treat the underlying causes of disease and aging rather than only managing symptoms.

This guide is a complete introduction to stem cell therapy. It explains what stem cells are and how they work, the different types and where they come from, the specific therapies offered, and what treatment involves from consultation to follow-up. The aim is to give you a clear, grounded understanding so you can make informed decisions with your medical team.

Stem cell therapy is rarely used in isolation. It is tailored to the individual and often combined with supporting therapies for a coordinated effect. Throughout, the focus is on the science, the practical reality of treatment, and honest expectations.

A note on evidence: cellular medicine is advancing rapidly, and the strength of evidence varies by condition. Descriptions here explain the intended rationale and typical use of stem cell therapy rather than promising specific outcomes. This is general educational information and does not replace a personal medical consultation, diagnosis, or treatment plan.

Part I

Understanding Stem Cells

The biology behind regenerative medicine — what stem cells are, and how they repair the body.

What Are Stem Cells?

Stem cells are the body's raw material — unspecialised cells from which all other specialised cells are generated. Unlike a mature nerve, muscle, or blood cell, a stem cell has not yet committed to a specific job, which gives it a unique flexibility that the body draws on to build, maintain, and repair tissue throughout life.

Two defining properties set stem cells apart. The first is self-renewal: the ability to divide and produce more stem cells, maintaining a reserve — achieved through symmetric division (producing two stem cells) and asymmetric division (producing one stem cell and one cell that goes on to specialise). The second is differentiation: the ability to mature into specialised cell types, often in stages, passing through a more restricted progenitor stage before becoming a fully specialised bone, cartilage, muscle, or nerve cell. Together, these properties make stem cells the foundation of the body's repair systems — and of regenerative medicine.

In everyday life, stem cells quietly replace cells that are lost to normal wear, injury, or disease. When tissue is damaged, they can be recruited to the site to help rebuild it. Regenerative therapy harnesses this natural capacity — supplying stem cells, or supporting the body's own — to enhance repair where age or illness has reduced it.

Because of their versatility and powerful effects, stem cells are being studied and used across an enormous range of conditions, from orthopedic injuries to neurological, cardiac, and autoimmune disease.

Stem Cell Potency & Types

Not all stem cells are equal. They differ in potency — the range of cell types they can become — and this determines how they are used in medicine.

Totipotent

Able to form every cell type plus supporting tissues; exist only at the very earliest stage of development.

Pluripotent

Able to become almost any cell type in the body; embryonic stem cells are the classic example.

Multipotent

Able to become a related family of cell types; mesenchymal stem cells are the most widely used in therapy.

Progenitor Cells

Further specialised, committed toward one particular lineage for rebuilding a specific tissue.

A second important distinction is between adult (or somatic) stem cells and other types. Adult stem cells — including the mesenchymal stem cells found in bone marrow, fat, umbilical cord, placenta, and dental pulp — are the mainstay of clinical regenerative therapy because they are accessible, versatile, and have a strong safety record. Their combination of multipotency, powerful signaling, and immune-modulating behaviour is exactly what makes them so useful.

In practice, the choice of cell type, potency, and source is matched to the treatment goal and the individual — a decision made during consultation and diagnostic assessment rather than by a fixed rule.

How Stem Cells Heal the Body

It is a common misconception that stem cells work simply by turning into new tissue. In reality, they heal through several complementary mechanisms working together — and for the mesenchymal stem cells most used in therapy, the signaling role is often the most important.

Paracrine Signaling

Stem cells release a rich mixture of growth factors, cytokines, and exosomes that instruct surrounding cells to repair — the single most important mechanism for MSCs.

Immunomodulation

They sense and rebalance an over- or under-active immune response, calming harmful inflammation.

Angiogenesis

They promote the formation of new blood vessels, restoring circulation and oxygen to damaged tissue.

Homing

They naturally migrate toward sites of injury and inflammation, concentrating their effect where it is needed.

Differentiation

Where appropriate, they mature into the specific cell types the tissue requires.

Anti-Fibrotic & Anti-Apoptotic

They help reduce scarring and prevent the premature death of stressed cells.

This multi-pathway action is why stem cell therapy can help across such a wide range of conditions: rather than doing one thing, the cells orchestrate a coordinated repair response tailored to the environment they find themselves in.

Why Stem Cells Decline with Age

Aging is, at its core, a cellular process — and the decline of stem cells is central to it. As we grow older, both the number and the activity of our stem cells diminish. Fewer stem cells are available to replace those lost to wear and injury, and those that remain divide and respond less efficiently. At the same time, cells accumulate damage: telomeres — the protective caps on our chromosomes — shorten with each division, mitochondria produce energy less efficiently, and molecular clutter such as advanced glycation end-products builds up.

The combined result is slower, less complete healing, reduced resilience, and a rising risk of age-related disease. Tissues that once repaired quickly recover more slowly, and low-grade inflammation tends to increase.

Regenerative and anti-aging medicine targets this decline directly — replenishing stem cells and their signals, and supporting the cellular systems on which repair depends — with the goal of restoring the body's capacity to heal.

Part II

Where Stem Cells Come From

The sources of stem cells used in therapy, and how the right one is chosen.

Autologous vs Allogeneic Sources

Stem cells used in therapy come from two broad categories, each with distinct advantages. The right choice depends on the condition, the treatment goal, the cells required, and the individual.

Autologous — from your own body

Autologous cells are collected from the patient and returned to the same person. Because they are the individual's own cells, rejection and allergic reaction are not concerns. The main considerations are that collection involves a minor procedure and that, with age or illness, a person's own cells may be fewer or less vigorous.

Allogeneic — from screened donor tissue

Allogeneic cells are obtained from carefully screened donor tissue, most often birth-associated tissues such as the umbilical cord or placenta that would otherwise be discarded. These cells are prized for being young, potent, and plentiful. Mesenchymal stem cells in particular are considered low-risk for rejection because of their immune-modulating properties, and all donor material is thoroughly screened for safety.

Autologous Sources

Stem cells collected from the patient's own body.

Bone Marrow-Derived (BMSCs)

The original and most extensively studied source. Bone marrow contains both hematopoietic (blood-forming) stem cells and mesenchymal stem cells, collected from the pelvic bone. Its long clinical track record makes it a well-understood, trusted source.

Peripheral Blood (PBSCs)

Stem cells mobilised out of the bone marrow and into the bloodstream, collected from a blood sample. This offers a less invasive alternative to a marrow harvest while still providing valuable stem cells.

Adipose Tissue-Derived (ADSCs)

Fat tissue is an abundant and accessible reservoir of mesenchymal stem cells, obtained in relatively large numbers through a minor procedure. This ease of access and high yield have made adipose tissue an increasingly popular autologous source.

Because all three come from the patient, they carry essentially no risk of immune rejection.

Allogeneic Sources

Stem cells from carefully screened donor tissue.

Amniotic Membrane (AMSCs)

Derived from the amniotic membrane surrounding the developing baby, these young cells have notable regenerative and anti-inflammatory properties and are valued for their potency.

Wharton's Jelly (WJSCs)

Sourced from the gelatinous connective tissue of the umbilical cord, these are among the most potent and readily proliferating mesenchymal stem cells. Their youth and vigour make them a highly regarded allogeneic source.

Dental Pulp (DPSCs)

Obtained from the soft pulp inside teeth, dental pulp stem cells are versatile and have shown particular potential for neural and dental tissue.

Umbilical Cord Blood (UCBSCs)

Collected from the blood of the umbilical cord, these cells are rich in hematopoietic stem cells and have a long, established history of use in cell therapy and transplantation.

All donor-derived cells are thoroughly screened, and mesenchymal types are considered low-risk for rejection thanks to their immune-regulating nature.

Choosing the Right Source

There is no single "best" stem cell source — the ideal choice depends on the individual and the goal of treatment. Several factors guide the decision:

  • The condition being treated — different tissues and goals may favour different cell types and sources.
  • The patient's age and health — younger, healthier individuals may have vigorous autologous cells, while donor cells offer youth and potency regardless of the recipient's age.
  • Cell type required — whether the goal calls for mesenchymal, hematopoietic, or organ-specific progenitors.
  • Quantity and potency needed — some sources yield more cells, or more potent cells, than others.
  • Practical considerations — the invasiveness of collection and the individual's preferences.

This decision is made together with your medical team, informed by consultation and diagnostic assessment, to arrive at the approach best suited to you.

Part III

Stem Cell Therapies

The specific stem cell treatments offered — what each is, and how it works.

Mesenchymal Stem Cells (MSCs)

The versatile workhorse of regenerative medicine.

MSCs are multipotent adult stem cells capable of becoming bone, cartilage, muscle, and fat cells. They are among the most widely used and studied cells in regenerative medicine because they are versatile, can be obtained from several tissues, and exert broad healing effects. Importantly, most of their benefit comes not from replacing tissue directly but from coordinating the body's own repair.

The science — how it works

MSCs act mainly through paracrine signaling — they secrete a rich mixture of growth factors, cytokines, and exosomes that reduce inflammation, regulate an over- or under-active immune system, promote new blood-vessel growth, and stimulate resident cells to regenerate. They also "home" toward sites of injury and inflammation, naturally concentrating their effect. This combination of immune modulation, anti-inflammatory action, and repair signaling explains their use across many different conditions.

Applications

  • Broad anti-inflammatory and immune-balancing support
  • Joint, cartilage, and soft-tissue repair
  • Autoimmune and inflammatory conditions
  • Support across many organ systems

Key benefits

  • Powerful, multi-pathway repair signaling
  • Immune-modulating effect for over-active immune conditions
  • Available from bone marrow, adipose tissue, umbilical cord, placenta, and dental pulp
  • Versatile delivery options

What to expect

After consultation and any needed testing, MSCs are delivered either intravenously for systemic effect or by local injection into a joint or specific tissue. The process is typically straightforward and well tolerated, with brief rest afterward and route-specific aftercare.

Organ-Based Stem Cell Therapy (SCT)

Targeted regeneration for a specific organ system.

Organ-based stem cell therapy directs regenerative cells to a specific organ or system rather than treating the body generically. The aim is to repair or replace cells lost to disease, injury, or aging in a focused way — whether the target is neurological, cardiac, pulmonary, hepatic, renal, or musculoskeletal.

How it works

Regenerative cells are selected to match the target condition and delivered by the route that best reaches the affected tissue. Once in place, they dampen harmful inflammation, release growth factors that instruct resident cells to repair, encourage new blood vessels to restore circulation, and help modulate the local immune response. In some settings they may differentiate into needed cell types.

Applications

  • Neurological and spinal-cord conditions
  • Heart and vascular disease
  • Lung, liver, kidney, and pancreatic disorders
  • Orthopedic and degenerative joint conditions
  • General age-related decline and tissue repair

Exosome Therapy — Enhanced MSCs

The regenerative power of stem cells, without the cells.

Exosomes are tiny lipid-bilayer vesicles — roughly 30 to 150 nanometres across — that cells use to communicate. Those released by MSCs carry proteins, lipids, mRNA, microRNA, and growth factors, reproducing many of the effects of stem cells as a cell-free therapy, with practical advantages.

How it works

When delivered into the body, MSC-exosomes fuse with recipient cells and transfer their bioactive cargo directly, switching on repair and anti-inflammatory pathways. Their extremely small size lets them reach tissues that whole cells cannot, including crossing the blood-brain barrier. Because no living cells are transplanted, there is no risk of the cells forming tumours, and exosomes are easier to standardise and dose.

Applications

  • Skin regeneration and aesthetic treatments
  • Wound healing and tissue repair
  • Neurological support
  • Amplifying and extending the effect of stem cell protocols

Organ-Specific Progenitor Stem Cells

Partly specialised cells for rebuilding one target tissue.

Progenitor cells sit one step beyond stem cells — already committed toward a particular tissue lineage, such as cardiac, hepatic, pancreatic, or neural. Because they are partially specialised, they can integrate into and regenerate a specific organ more directly than a general-purpose cell.

How it works

Once delivered, organ-specific progenitors continue maturing along their predetermined lineage and contribute directly to rebuilding the target tissue, while also supporting the surrounding repair environment with helpful signalling.

Applications

  • Focused regeneration of a single organ or tissue
  • Conditions where general support is not enough
  • Complement to systemic stem cell therapy
Part IV

Treatment & Care

Delivery, the treatment process, safety, and what to expect.

How Stem Cells Are Delivered

The way stem cells are administered is matched to the condition and the tissue being targeted, because reaching the right place is essential to a therapy's effect.

IV

Intravenous (IV) Infusion

Cells are delivered into the bloodstream for a systemic, body-wide effect — commonly used when the goal is broad anti-inflammatory and regenerative support.

IA

Intra-Articular Injection

Cells are injected directly into a joint to concentrate their effect on cartilage and joint tissue, used for orthopedic conditions such as osteoarthritis.

IT

Intrathecal / Intraspinal Injection

Cells are placed into the spinal canal to target neurological and spinal-cord conditions directly, close to the affected tissue.

IM

Intramuscular (IM) Injection

Cells or supporting agents are delivered into muscle for gradual absorption.

LT

Local / Targeted Injection

Cells are injected directly into a specific site of injury or damage for a focused regenerative effect.

The route — or combination of routes — is chosen individually as part of your treatment plan.

02

Delivery

06

Follow-Up & Monitoring

Conditions Addressed

Stem cell therapy is applied across a broad spectrum of conditions spanning the major organ systems. Every case begins with a personalised assessment of whether treatment is suitable and which approach fits best.

Brain, Spinal Cord & Neurodegenerative

From stroke and spinal-cord injury to Parkinson's, MS, and neuropathy.

Heart & Cardiovascular

Including heart failure and coronary and peripheral artery disease.

Lungs & Pulmonary

Such as COPD, pulmonary fibrosis, and interstitial lung disease.

Liver, Kidney & Pancreas

Including cirrhosis, chronic kidney disease, and diabetes.

Autoimmune & Connective Tissue

Such as rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, and scleroderma.

Musculoskeletal & Orthopedic

Osteoarthritis, cartilage and tendon injuries, and sports injuries.

Skin & Wound Healing

From chronic wounds and burns to aesthetic rejuvenation.

Other Complex Conditions

Including eye, reproductive, ENT, endocrine, and blood disorders.

A detailed consultation and diagnostic assessment determine suitability and the most appropriate personalised approach.

Safety, Quality & Sourcing

Stem cell therapy is powerful, and delivering it safely depends on rigorous standards at every step. Quality and safety are the foundation of responsible care.

Careful Sourcing and Preparation

Cells are obtained and prepared to high standards, and donor material is thoroughly screened for safety.

Qualified, Supervised Delivery

Treatments are administered by trained practitioners under medical supervision, using the appropriate route and protocol.

Personalised Screening

Every treatment is preceded by assessment and, where relevant, testing to confirm suitability and identify anything requiring caution.

Ongoing Monitoring

Progress and wellbeing are followed through the course of treatment, and plans are adjusted based on response.

Honest Communication

Realistic, transparent discussion of what stem cell therapy can and cannot do, so expectations are grounded and decisions informed.

Designing a Personalised Protocol

No two patients are the same, so stem cell treatment is never one-size-fits-all. A personalised protocol brings together the right cells, source, dose, delivery route, and timing for a specific person and goal — informed by consultation and diagnostic findings.

Stem cell therapy is also frequently combined with supporting treatments that work through complementary mechanisms — for example, therapies that support the healing environment, reduce a harmful burden beforehand, or aid functional recovery afterward. Sequencing and spacing these elements thoughtfully often matters as much as the cells themselves.

Protocols are dynamic. Progress is monitored through follow-up assessments and repeat testing, and the plan is refined based on how the individual responds — a start-with-a-baseline, treat, measure, and refine approach that is central to safe and effective regenerative care.

Glossary of Key Terms

Stem Cell — An unspecialised cell that can renew itself and develop into specialised cell types.
Self-Renewal — A stem cell's ability to divide and produce more stem cells.
Differentiation — The process by which a stem cell matures into a specialised cell type.
Potency — How many cell types a stem cell can become — totipotent, pluripotent, or multipotent.
Mesenchymal Stem Cells (MSCs) — Multipotent adult stem cells that repair tissue largely through signaling and immune modulation.
Hematopoietic Stem Cells — Blood-forming stem cells that give rise to red cells, white cells, and platelets.
Progenitor Cells — Partly specialised cells already committed toward a particular tissue lineage.
Exosomes — Tiny vesicles that carry regenerative signals between cells, used as a cell-free therapy.
Growth Factors — Signaling proteins that instruct cells to divide, migrate, and repair tissue.
Cytokines — Signaling molecules that regulate inflammation and immune activity.
Paracrine Signaling — Cells influencing nearby cells by releasing signaling molecules.
Autologous — Derived from your own body; carries essentially no risk of immune rejection.
Allogeneic — Derived from a screened donor; valued for potency and immune-modulating properties.
Immunomodulation — Adjusting an over- or under-active immune response toward balance.
Angiogenesis — The formation of new blood vessels.
Wharton's Jelly — The gelatinous connective tissue of the umbilical cord, a potent source of MSCs.
